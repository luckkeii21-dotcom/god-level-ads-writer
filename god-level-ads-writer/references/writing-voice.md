# Writing Voice — Anti-AI Rules, Voice DNA, Human Writing Principles

_Last updated: 2026-04 | Source: Linguistic research + practitioner analysis (Human vs AI Writing PDF)_

---

## WHY THIS FILE EXISTS

Most AI-generated ad copy is polished but lifeless. It reads like a corporate brochure written
by a committee. It triggers the "this is a bot" alarm in every reader who has seen enough of it.

The goal of this skill is writing that sounds like it came from a confident, specific, slightly
blunt human who has been in the room. Not from a language model that averaged a million
marketing emails.

This file encodes what separates the two.

---

## HARD BANS — NEVER WRITE THESE

### Banned Constructions

**Em dashes**
Never use — this. Ever. Not in scripts, not in copy, not in notes.
Use a comma, a period, or a short new sentence instead.

**Contrast-negation patterns**
These are the most common AI tell in ad writing. Never write:
- "It's not just X, it's Y"
- "This isn't about X, it's about Y"
- "Not just another X"
- "This goes beyond X"
- "More than just X"
- "X isn't enough — you need Y" (also uses em dash, double banned)

Instead: state the thing directly. If Y is the point, say Y. Don't frame it against X.

Bad: "This isn't just a dental clinic. It's a place where you feel safe."
Good: "You walk in nervous. You leave with a straight smile and zero anxiety about coming back."

**Stock transitions (paragraph openers)**
These make every paragraph sound like a listicle:
- "Furthermore,"
- "Moreover,"
- "In addition,"
- "Additionally,"
- "In today's world,"
- "At its core,"
- "At the end of the day,"
- "It goes without saying,"
- "First and foremost,"
- "Last but not least,"

**Overused AI phrases**
These words appear disproportionately in AI-generated text. Avoid:
- "delve into"
- "harness"
- "underscore"
- "pivotal"
- "transformative"
- "game-changer"
- "revolutionary"
- "cutting-edge"
- "unlock"
- "unleash"
- "leverage" (as filler, not as a tool reference)
- "utilize" (say "use")
- "commence" (say "start")
- "in order to" (say "to")
- "due to the fact that" (say "because")

**Balanced safe opinions**
AI defaults to "on one hand... on the other hand." It presents both sides to avoid
taking a position. Real writers take positions.

Bad: "Some people find this approach works well, while others prefer a more traditional method."
Good: "Cold outreach works. For a while. Until your reply rate hits zero and you realize you've
been paying to annoy people."

## WHAT GREAT WRITING DOES INSTEAD

### Sentence Rhythm

Human writers vary sentence length deliberately. AI produces medium-long sentences at a
steady, uniform pace.

Read the rhythm out loud. If every sentence lands at the same beat, break the pattern.

Good rhythm example:
> "You've tried everything. The pills, the programs, the meal plans your trainer sent you
> at midnight after you texted in a panic. Nothing stuck. Here's why."

Bad rhythm example:
> "Many people struggle to find solutions that work for their specific needs, and they often
> try various approaches before discovering what is truly effective for them."

One punchy sentence. A longer one that builds context and weight. Short again to land.
Repeat in variation. Never in a formula.

### Specificity

Vague writing is the enemy of trust. The reader's brain is asking: "Is this real?"
Specifics say yes.

**Numbers**: "3 clients in 11 days" beats "multiple clients quickly." Always.
**Names**: "Marcus from Phoenix" beats "one of our clients." Always.
**Dates and times**: "Last Tuesday" beats "recently." Always.
**Dollar amounts**: "$4,200 in 6 days" beats "significant revenue." Always.
**Places**: "Our clinic in Bandra" beats "our location." Always.

Every substantial piece of copy should contain at least one specific detail that could only
exist if the story were real.

### Concrete Active Verbs

Weak: "She experienced a transformation in her confidence levels."
Strong: "She walked into that interview and got the job."

Weak: "Our system helps businesses achieve greater operational efficiency."
Strong: "You spend 40 minutes on leads. This handles it in 3."

The verb should do the work. Not the adjective. Not the adverb. The verb.

### Voice and Stance

Great writing has a perspective. It takes a position. It does not hedge.

It can be:
- Blunt and direct ("Most coaches are selling you a fantasy. Here's what actually works.")
- Warm and personal ("I remember the first time a patient cried in my chair. Not from pain. From relief.")
- Dry and slightly irreverent ("It's a dental cleaning. Not a spa day. But we've had people fall asleep.")
- Energetic and specific ("47 leads. One week. One ad. That's the number.")

What it cannot be: neutral, balanced, and safe. That is the corporate voice. Not this one.

## THE FIVE HUMAN WRITING TRAITS

From comparative linguistic research on human vs. AI text:

### 1. Emotional Range

AI writing is consistently positive, motivational, and polished.
Human writing includes the full range: frustration, doubt, disappointment, humor, bluntness.

Ads that acknowledge real negative emotion build more trust than ads that are entirely cheerful.

Example: "You're not lazy. The reason the other programs didn't work is that they were
designed for people who already had two hours a day to spend on this. You don't."

### 2. Personal and Temporal References

AI stays abstract. Humans say "last Tuesday," "my neighbor," "when I was 29."

Use time-specific references. Use person-specific references. Make it feel like a real account
of a real experience, not a case study summary.

### 3. Sentence Structure Variation

AI produces predictable syntactic patterns: main clause + comma + participial phrase, repeated.

Human writers break patterns. Start a sentence with "And." Let a paragraph be one line.
Write a sentence that runs longer than expected because the idea earns it. Then stop.

### 4. Lexical Diversity

AI reuses the same vocabulary. Humans reach for the right word, not the available one.

If you find yourself writing "amazing results" — what does amazing mean here? Is it 40 lbs?
Is it a $12,000 month? Is it being able to walk without pain for the first time in a year?
Say that instead.

### 5. Idiosyncratic Transitions

Not "Furthermore," but "Here's the thing." Not "In addition," but "That's the first problem.
The second one is worse." Not "However," but a new paragraph that contradicts the previous one
without announcing the contradiction.

## GRAMMAR STANDARDS

Excellent grammar is non-negotiable. The anti-AI rules do not mean sloppy.

- No typos. None.
- No missing punctuation in places that need it.
- No run-on sentences that lose the reader.
- Comma use must be correct. If you are not sure, use two short sentences.
- Contractions are fine and often preferred ("you're," "it's," "don't").
- Sentence fragments are acceptable when they serve the rhythm and the intent is clear.
- Never use "they're/their/there," "your/you're," "its/it's" incorrectly.

The test: would a sharp, well-read person wince reading this? If yes, fix it.

## SIDE-BY-SIDE: AI WRITING VS. THIS SKILL'S STANDARD

| Dimension | Typical AI Output | This Skill's Standard |
|-----------|------------------|----------------------|
| Tone | Formal, consistently positive, motivational | Varies — direct, specific, occasionally blunt |
| Sentence rhythm | Narrow range, medium-long, uniform pace | Wide variation — short punchy lines next to longer ones |
| Vocabulary | Generic, formal ("utilize," "leverage") | Specific, concrete, active verbs |
| Emotion | Polite, avoids extremes | Full range — frustration, relief, confidence, doubt |
| Structure | Predictable transitions, list-like | Flexible, unexpected turns, occasional rule-breaking |
| Specificity | Abstract general advice | Numbers, names, places, dates |
| Em dashes | Everywhere | Never |
| Contrast patterns | "Not just X, it's Y" | Banned entirely — state the point directly |

## QUICK SELF-CHECK BEFORE DELIVERING

Read the output. Ask these five questions:

1. Does any sentence sound like it came from a press release? Rewrite it.
2. Is there at least one specific number, name, or time reference? If not, add one.
3. Do three or more consecutive sentences have the same approximate length? Break the pattern.
4. Does the copy take a clear position? Or does it hedge and balance? If it hedges, cut the hedge.
5. Would a real person actually say this out loud? If not, rewrite it.

If all five pass, the copy is ready.
